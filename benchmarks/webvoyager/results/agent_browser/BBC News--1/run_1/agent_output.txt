**Article:** *"'Pregorexia': The hidden struggle of eating disorders facing pregnant women"* — BBC Health/Future, published 16–17 Jun 2026 (the most recent dated article in the section).

**Key points:**
- **"Pregorexia" is real but overlooked:** ~1 in 20 women experience an eating disorder during pregnancy (5–7.5% meet diagnostic criteria), and ~13% of postpartum mothers do — figures researchers believe are underestimates due to stigma and lack of screening. Some relapse from a prior disorder; others develop symptoms for the first time.
- **Why pregnancy is a "perfect storm":** Rapid hormonal shifts, metabolism changes, weight gain, sleep loss and a perceived "loss of control" hit during one of three vulnerable windows for women (puberty, pregnancy, perimenopause). ~70% of pregnant/postpartum women report body-image dissatisfaction.
- **Serious physical risks:** The body prioritizes the baby, risking maternal nutrient deficiency and bone/muscle loss. Anorexia and bulimia nearly double pregnancy complications (severe nausea/vomiting, bleeding, low haemoglobin) and are linked to miscarriage, low birth weight, premature birth and possible developmental issues. Birth weight is a key predictor of the child's lifelong health.
- **Postpartum is a danger zone:** Women who hold off relapse during pregnancy often see it return after birth, driven by hormones, sleep deprivation and pressure to "bounce back." One case (Courtney Louise) describes becoming suicidal postpartum.
- **It goes undetected:** Symptoms overlap with normal pregnancy (e.g. morning sickness vs. purging); only ~10% of pregnant women with bulimia are identified, and just half are referred. Patients self-stigmatize, fear judgment ("Don't you love your baby enough?"), and often get no support even when they disclose.
- **Hope and solutions:** Experts (Sharp, Galbally, Shanti, Claydon) stress pregnancy can also be a powerful motivator for lasting recovery. New resources exist (2022 clinical guidelines for anorexia in pregnancy; support orgs), though no tailored medications/treatments yet — clinicians use generic therapy, support groups and antidepressants. Recommended changes: non-judgmental collaborative care, withholding weight numbers during check-ups, and patients confiding in a trusted person. As one expert puts it: *"Everybody has an eating disorder alone, but nobody recovers alone."*
